The Epic of Nanni

Long before the time of zero-star reviews, in the land of Sumer, there lived a merchant named Nanni
One day, Nanni decided to send a message to Ea-nasir, a copper merchant who had sold him some low-quality copper:

When you came, you said to me as follows :
'I will give Gimil-Sin (when he comes) fine quality copper ingots.'
You left then but you did not do what you promised me.
You put ingots which were not good before my messenger (Sit-Sin) and said:
' If you want to take them, take them; if you do not want to take them, go away!'

After receiving Nanni's letter, Ea-nasir sent a response that was anything but satisfactory. He insulted Nanni and claimed that the copper was of the highest quality.
In response, Nanni wrote another letter, which is famous for its colorful language and memorable insults.
He accused Ea-nasir of being a cheat and a liar and threatened to take his case to the king. Nanni also warned Ea-nasir that he would spread the word about his dishonesty, saying:

You will know what kind of person I am, when I send my messenger to collect the good money.

The letter ends with Nanni telling Ea-nasir that he will never do business with him again.

Ea-Nasir's greed

Despite Nanni's threats, it is not clear what happened next.

We don't know if he was able to collect his money or take his case to the king. Ea-nasir might have ignored him, or made a reply now lost in time.
Nanni likely didn't know his complaint would be made immortal, but at brF we think it would please him to get a last laugh that echoes for all eternity.

We have ancient texts that feature kings, priests, and other elites, but few on the challenges faced by merchants and ordinary people.
Nanni's letters give us a rare 4,000+ year glimpse into the world of ancient Sumerian commerce, and stand as a testament to timeless power of a well-worded complaint.
